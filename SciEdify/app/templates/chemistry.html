Periodic table

The periodic table of the chemical elements is an arrangement of the elements with seven periods (rows) and eighteen columns (groups). There are eight main groups and ten transition groups. The first widely accepted table was the one made by the Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev in 1869. Today there are a hundred and eighteen elements. They are placed in table cells, in reading order of ascending atomic number. This number indicates how many protons accordingly electrons the element has. The group shows how many electrons the element has in its outermost (valence) shell and the period – the number of electron shells in an element. Every element has a one- or two-letter abbreviation. Between the second and the third groups of the table there are two series of elements – lanthanoids and actinoids because they do not fit anywhere else.
